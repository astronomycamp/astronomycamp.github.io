Surprising Spinoffs from Astronomy Camps
The Astronomy Camps began in 1998, with Dr. Ray White (Astronomy Professor) and Ms. Joan Morrill (Director of Lifelong Learning and Travel at the UArizona Alumni Association). The first Camp included 21 teenage children of University alumni and was held at the Catalina Observatories at the summit of Mt. Lemmon (9200 feet), north of Tucson. Afterwards, Dr. White concluded that he didn't enjoy working with teenagers and offered Dr. McCarthy the opportunity. Don was already including teenagers in actual research, both at the MMT Observatory and at Sahuaro School with veteran teacher Jeff Lockwood. The concept of "research-based science education" expanded rapidly.

Since 1998, many different types of Camps were created, such as Beginning/Advanced versions for Teens and Adults; an Educators Camp; Camps for schools around the U.S. and Mexico; the Girls Scouts of the USA; Camps for local groups (UArizona Honors College; Pima Community College,); and special events to support both faculty research grants, College of Science outreach (e.g., Hands Across Borders; Galileo Circle), as well as the local community (e.g., Davis Monthan Air Force Base, Tucson’s Boys and Girls Clubs).

The Camps have benefitted UArizona, its faculty, and students:

  • ~70 Camp alumni enrolled as undergraduate and graduate students, postdocs, and faculty at UArizona.
    • Others became essential technical staff at five observatories throughout Arizona.
  • The family of a Teen Camper funded ...
    • a new dome now housing a 40-inch telescope used for asteroid recovery by the Catalina Sky Survey.
    • $20M to the UArizona Mirror Lab and Giant Magellan Telescope.
  • Camp supported a postdoc's NSF CAREER proposal to fund ...
    • a new dome for the 20" Jamieson Telescope, now the SkyCenter's 24" Phillips telescope.
  • An Adult Camper funded ...
    • ~30% of the annual operating costs of Mt. Lemmon Observatory for several years.
    • the creation of the Mt. Lemmon SkyCenter (MLSC)
  • Astronomy Camp provided the heart of the education proposal for UArizona's NIRCam project (~$1M) for the James Webb Space Telescope.
    • This 20-year partnership with the Girl Scouts of the USA "Trained the Trainers" (400 from 47 states) so young women could engage in hands-on STEM activities related to JWST.
    • A former UArizona President donated $250K for improvement of Camp facilities on Mt. Lemmon.
  • Astronomy Camp itself funded ...
    • computer hardware to control the 60" telescope on Mt. Lemmon.
    • hardware improvements for Internet bandwidth, A/V, and kitchen equipment at the MLSC.

As the Camps continued to inspire, and invest in, people both young and old, unexpected outcomes began happening. Below are some events Dr. McCarthy (the Director) never anticipated:

  1. Children of alumni attending the Teen Camps.
  2. Parents and children attending together.
  3. Children of UArizona faculty and staff attending.
  4. Parents stunned that the Camps are “life-changing.” “We want you to know what your Camp did for our child.”
  5. Campers returning as “changed” people.
  6. Teen Campers marrying each other ~12 years later.
  7. Campers initiating Facebook group, reunions, and special trips to reconnect.
  8. Campers inspiring their families (parents and siblings) to attend.
  9. Hosting a faux-graduation for a high-school senior choosing to attend Camp instead of his high school graduation with Neil deGrass Tyson as a featured speaker.
  10. Staff members meeting future spouse, including my own daughter.
  11. A staff member proposing marriage to another staff member at sunrise on Mt. Lemmon.
  12. Adult Campers becoming trained staff members at the Teen Camps.
  13. Inspirational, light-bulb moments at Camps.
  14. Helping Campers overcome fears: Home sickness, high altitude, anxiety, moths, Daddy-longlegs, the “Death March,” ….
  15. Helping Campers learn about hygiene.
  16. Working alongside Campers to cook and clean.
  17. A Campers in tears because Camp was ending soon and no one at home understood.
  18. Having to break bad news to Teen Campers about deaths with family and friends.
  19. Campers writing popular articles about Camp in prominent magazines.
  20. A Camp graduate developing a special, long-lasting Camp for her Mexican-based school.
  21. Campers (Teen and Adult) discovering new supernovae and Trans-Neptunian Objects.
  22. Articles about the Teen Camps in National Geographic World, and Arizona Highways.
  23. Press interviews with Campers, live from the 61” telescope.
  24. Real-time Q&A with astronauts in the orbiting International Space Station with live press coverage on Tohono O’Odham radio station (KOHN) with lead broadcasting by a former Teen Camper.
  25. Teen Campers changing their course loads to emphasize STEM education.
  26. Teen Campers choosing their colleges based on mentoring from our staff.
  27. Campers winning prominent, national awards for their research.
  28. Campers enrolling at UArizona to study a wide variety of STEM majors.
  29. Campers becoming UArizona’s top scholars/researchers and winning awards from the College of Science.
